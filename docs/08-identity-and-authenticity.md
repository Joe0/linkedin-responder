# Identity, Authenticity, and Rhetorical Persuasion

The mechanics of negotiation are highly dependent on the behavioral profile and authentic identity of the candidate. Attempting to execute a script that violates a professional's core persona triggers a breakdown in trust, credibility, and ultimately the deal itself.

## The Danger of False Personas and the Rescinded Offer

**Case study:** A female Chief Marketing Officer had a massive $800,000 offer rescinded. Advised by an external career coach to negotiate "like an aggressive white man," she deployed abrasive, confrontational tactics that completely alienated the female-led hiring committee. The behavior was entirely incongruent with her known collaborative professional persona, and the company perceived the sudden aggression as a massive red flag regarding her future leadership style.

**Consequence:** Offer rescinded.

**Recovery:** She had to use extreme ownership and Chris Voss's principles of tactical empathy. She contacted the CEO, admitted total vulnerability, and explicitly stated:

> *"In the past I've failed... I hired a coach... I made a mistake."*

## The Shared Identity Tribe Hack

Following the admission of fault, she utilized the "Shared Identity Tribe" hack — bridging the emotional gap by appealing to a shared demographic or experiential background. In this case: their shared experience as female executives navigating historically male-dominated corporate environments.

This raw authenticity and transparency completely disarmed the CEO, repairing the fractured relationship and successfully reinstating the massive contract.

## Implications for Minority Groups and Women

Minority groups and women have a profound ethical responsibility to negotiate aggressively — to continuously raise the compensation precedent for those who follow:

> *"Whatever precedent you help me deliver, you're going to get two to three times that."*

However, these maneuvers must be executed using frameworks that **amplify authentic strengths**, not mimic poorly fitting, aggressive corporate stereotypes. Success is found in calculated, collaborative strategy — not in performing an identity that is not your own.
