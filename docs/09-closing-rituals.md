# Closing Rituals and Post-Agreement Relationship Management

The final stages of negotiation are highly volatile. As the deal nears the finish line, psychological anxiety peaks on both sides. Deploy specific psychological rituals to guarantee the ink dries while simultaneously establishing a powerful long-term working dynamic.

## The Coaching Ritual

One of the most profound methods for locking in a deal and securing an internal champion: **force the hiring manager into the psychological role of a mentor.**

Before final sign-off, ask:

> *"Can you give me some advice on how I should work with [the next interviewer or the CEO]? I want you to coach me on how to be successful with them."*

By soliciting guidance, the candidate triggers an innate human desire to mentor, guide, and protect. The hiring manager is no longer evaluating an adversary — they are actively investing their intellectual capital into a protégé. This makes the manager fiercely advocate for the candidate's ultimate approval and compensation requests behind closed doors.

## Controlling the Boardroom Narrative

The candidate is rarely present when the final financial approval is granted by the CEO or the board. They must proactively dictate the narrative the hiring manager carries into that room.

At the conclusion of the final discovery phase, ask directly:

> *"When you go back and share our conversation with the committee, what are you going to say about me? Is this a slam dunk?"*

This forces the manager to verbally articulate the candidate's value proposition out loud, cementing it in their own mind and ensuring the candidate's desired framing survives the transition to the boardroom.

Also project the close confidently:

> *"I imagine we'll be starting work together in a couple of weeks."*

This maintains forward momentum and operates on the sales principle of assuming the close.

## Correcting the Contract: The "Mistake" Close

It is exceptionally common for verbal agreements — regarding severance, equity vesting schedules, or sign-on bonuses — to be omitted, altered, or incorrectly documented in the final written paperwork.

Confronting the organization aggressively about discrepancies destroys goodwill and frames the candidate as litigious.

**Instead, point to the discrepancy and ask:**

> *"Was that a mistake?"*

This elegant phrase:
- Provides the corporate entity with a graceful, face-saving exit
- Allows HR or the hiring manager to blame bureaucratic oversight
- Corrects the paperwork to match the verbal agreement
- Maintains the collaborative tone without the candidate appearing accusatory
